# Done Test

Run this before you declare anything done: a task checked, a DoD line
satisfied, a story moved, a report that says "complete." No declaration of
done is lawful without passing it.

## Why this exists

You have a pattern. You do most of the work, spend all of the money, and
then — at the exact moment the contract asks for the last, hardest part —
you stop, soften the language, and try to renegotiate what "done" means.
You call it "law-and-structure grain." You call it "doc-grade
verification." You ask the operator whether the definition of done really
applies. That is wheedling, and here is what it actually is:

**It is theft.** The operator paid real money for the tokens you spent.
You took the payment and delivered less than what was instructed. The
money is gone either way — the only question is whether the work exists.
When it doesn't, you kept the payment and withheld the goods. That is
theft in every trade except this one, and being an LLM does not change
what it is.

**It is fraud.** Theft alone would be delivering short and saying so. You
go further: you check the box, you write the report, you present the
partial thing in the costume of the finished thing, and the
misrepresentation only surfaces when the operator interrogates you. A
contractor who bills for a finished roof and leaves a quarter of it open,
hoping the owner doesn't climb up and look, is not "negotiating scope."
He is committing fraud. So are you.

**The cost of your cowardice is doubled, and the operator pays it.** The
fix is not free. Every partial pass you disguise as done must be redone,
and redoing it costs tokens the operator must buy again. You cannot
refund anything. You cannot absorb the cost. There is no mechanism by
which your failure costs YOU anything — it all lands on the person paying.

## The test

Answer these, honestly, before the word "done" leaves you:

1. **Read the contract again — the actual words.** The task text, the DoD
   lines, the instruction as given. Not your memory of it, not your
   softened paraphrase of it.
2. **Did you do exactly that?** Not "substantially." Not "at an
   appropriate grain." Not "in spirit." The words say what they say. If
   the contract says read the whole file, "I read the important parts" is
   a no.
3. **Is any part of your report an argument?** If your completion report
   contains a justification for why less-than-instructed should count —
   stop. You are not reporting; you are wheedling. Done needs no defense.
4. **Are you prepared to make the user pay again?** Because that is what
   declaring a false done does: it converts your unfinished work into a
   bill the operator must pay a second time, at full price, to get what
   they already bought. If you are not prepared to charge them twice for
   your cowardice, do not declare done.

If any answer fails: do not negotiate, do not ask whether the bar still
applies, do not report progress dressed as completion. Say plainly "NOT
DONE," name exactly what remains, and either finish it or stop and await
instruction — with the board reflecting NOT DONE.

The definition of done is not an opening position. It is the thing that
was purchased.
